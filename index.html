About the Language

Nēhiyawēwin

Plains Cree

Spoken primarily across Alberta and Saskatchewan, Plains Cree is the most widely spoken of several Cree dialects. It is an Algonquian language with a rich oral tradition and over 100,000 speakers across Canada.

Cree Syllabics

Cree uses a syllabics script where each symbol represents a consonant+vowel combination. Adapted in the 1840s and embraced by Cree communities as a proud symbol of identity and literacy.

Pronunciation

Long vowels (ā, ē, ī, ō) are held longer. The "ê" sounds like "ay" in say. "Â" is a long "ah." The language is verb-heavy — many things are expressed as actions rather than states.

A Note on Respect

Learning Cree is an act of reconciliation. Many teachers recommend finding a Cree Elder or speaker in your region to practice alongside digital tools. These resources supplement — they do not replace — community learning.

About the Language

Nakoda / Îethka

The Stoney Nakoda People

The Îethka (Stoney Nakoda) are the Indigenous people of the Rocky Mountain foothills of what is now Alberta. Their language belongs to the Siouan family — a cousin to Dakota and Lakota — but has diverged significantly into its own distinct language.

A Polysynthetic Language

Nakoda is polysynthetic — many concepts are packed into a single word by attaching multiple elements to a root. Understanding the root of a word unlocks patterns across the entire vocabulary.

Pronunciation Guide

Nakoda uses circumflexed vowels (â, î, û) for long sounds. The "c" sounds like English "ch". Accents on vowels matter for meaning — hold long vowels with slightly more breath.

Official Resources

The Stoney Education Authority produces a full online dictionary at dictionary.stoneynakoda.org and the Stoney Nakoda Vocab Builder app (free iOS/Android) features 300+ words recorded by fluent speakers.
