Background

About the Cree Language

Nēhiyawēwin is a living language, spoken by over 100,000 people across Canada — from Alberta to Manitoba, from the boreal forest to the prairie. It is an Algonquian language with a rich oral tradition and a unique writing system called Syllabics.

Plains Cree (nēhiyawēwin)

This site focuses on Plains Cree, spoken primarily in Alberta and Saskatchewan. It is one of the most widely spoken dialects and has extensive teaching resources developed by Cree scholars and communities.

Cree Syllabics

Developed in the 1840s by James Evans, syllabics is a writing system uniquely adapted to Cree sounds. Each symbol represents a consonant+vowel pair. Cree communities have embraced it as a proud part of their identity.

Pronunciation Guide

Cree uses long vowels (ā, ē, ī, ō) marked with a macron or circumflex. The "ê" sound is like "ay" in "say". The "â" sounds like "ah" held longer. Take it slowly — the sounds are musical once you feel them.

Respect & Protocol

Language carries culture. Learning Cree is an act of respect for Cree peoples and their knowledge systems. Many teachers recommend finding a Cree speaker in your community to practice with alongside digital tools.

Lesson 01 · Plains Cree

First Words — tânisi!

Greetings, numbers, and introducing yourself in Nēhiyawēwin

Introduction

Welcome to Nēhiyawēwin

Plains Cree (nēhiyawēwin) is spoken by the nêhiyawak — the Cree People — across the prairies and parklands of what is now Alberta and Saskatchewan. Before we begin, let's take a moment to appreciate that every word you learn is a step toward connecting with a living culture.

In this lesson you will learn:

  • How to say hello and ask how someone is
  • Numbers 1–5
  • How to say your name
  • A short quiz to test what you've learned
Vocabulary · Greetings

Saying Hello

The most important word you'll ever learn in Cree. tânisi is the everyday greeting — like "hello" or "how are you" rolled into one. Use it warmly and often.

ᑕᓂᓯ
tânisi
Hello / How are you?
namôya nânitaw, êkwa kiya?
namôya nânitaw, êkwa kiya?
I'm fine / not bad, and you?
ᐊᕼᑕᐤ
êkosi
OK / That's all / Goodbye (informal)

💡 Pronunciation tip: "tâ" rhymes with "saw". The "nisi" sounds like "knee-see". Try saying it slowly: TAW-ni-see.

Phrases · Introducing Yourself

Saying Your Name

In Cree, you introduce yourself with the phrase niya ____ nitisiyihkâson — which means "My name is ____." Try substituting your own name!

niya ____ nitisiyihkâson
niya [your name] nitisiyihkâson
My name is [your name]
tânisi kîspin kititisiyihkâson?
tânisi kîspin kititisiyihkâson?
What is your name?
tânitê ohci kiya?
tânitê ohci kiya?
Where are you from?

🌿 The word niya means "I / me" and kiya means "you." You'll see these a lot!
