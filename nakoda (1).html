Nakoda
Nakoda (also called Assiniboine or Nakota) is a critically endangered Siouan language spoken in southern Saskatchewan and northern Montana. Saskatchewan communities include Carry the Kettle, Pheasant Rump, White Bear, Ocean Man, and Mosquito Grizzly Bear's Head First Nations. This language is distinct from Stoney Nakoda (Alberta) — related, but not mutually intelligible.

Nasal Vowels — A Key Feature of Nakoda
What is a nasal vowel?

Nasal vowels are pronounced with air flowing through both the mouth and the nose simultaneously. They do not exist in English but are familiar from French — the vowels in bon and Jean are nasal. In Nakoda, nasal vowels are written with an ogonek, a small tail beneath the letter: ą, į, ų. These change word meaning — they are not optional decoration.

ą
nasal a — like French "an"
į
nasal i — like French "in"
ų
nasal u — like French "un"

The D-N-L Rule
Nakoda's place in the Dakotan language family

The Dakotan language family has a regular sound shift: where Dakota uses "d" and Lakota uses "l," Nakoda uses "n." This is why the people call themselves Nakoda (not Dakoda or Lakoda). This pattern runs through vocabulary: for example, the word for "ally/friend" is Dakota, Lakota, and Nakoda in each language respectively. Knowing this rule helps learners recognise relationships between cognate words across the three languages.

Word Stress
Stress changes meaning

Word stress is significant in Nakoda. The word eya with stress on the second syllable means "say," while the same word stressed on the first syllable means "take." Native speakers rarely mark stress in writing, just as English speakers don't. Linguists sometimes use an acute accent mark (e.g. eyá) to show the stressed syllable.

How Nakoda Grammar Works — an overview
SOV word order

Nakoda follows Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) word order as its base structure — the opposite of English (SVO). In Nakoda, "the man saw the dog" would follow the pattern "the man the dog saw." This is the default order for out-of-context sentences, though elements can move for emphasis or focus.

Agglutinative structure

Nakoda is agglutinative — meaning it builds complex meanings by attaching multiple prefixes and suffixes to root words. A single Nakoda verb can carry information about who is doing the action, to whom, when, and how — all packed into one word. This is very different from English, which uses separate words for each of these ideas.

No articles, no case, no tense marking

Three things English learners must unlearn when approaching Nakoda: there are no definite or indefinite articles (no "the" or "a"), there is no nominal case system (no different word forms for subject vs object), and there is no grammatical tense marking on verbs in the way English has past/present/future. Instead, Nakoda uses a verbal enclitic to produce a future/non-future distinction. Time is typically expressed through time words and context.

Source: Wikipedia, Assiniboine language article; Cumberland, Linda A. A Grammar of Nakoda (Assiniboine), University of Nebraska Press.

Nasal vowels in these words

wįca (man) and wįyą (woman) both begin with wį- — that nasal "i" is part of the root. hąwi (moon) begins with a nasal "a." These must be pronounced correctly, with nasal resonance.

Nasal vowels in numbers

Several numbers contain nasal vowels: wąži (1) has ą, nųba (2) has ų, zaptą (5) has ą, šaknoğą (8) has ą, napcuwąga (9) has ą. Pronouncing these with nasal resonance is part of saying the numbers correctly.

Horse — šųgatąga ("big dog")

The Nakoda word for horse combines šųga (dog) with a suffix meaning large — "big dog." Horses arrived later than dogs, and many Plains languages named them this way. The word contains two nasal vowels: ų and ą.

How Nakoda groups colors

Like Plains Cree, Nakoda uses a single word — to — to cover both blue and green. These are not considered separate color categories the way they are in English. This reflects a different way of organizing the visual spectrum, and is common across many languages worldwide. Neither system is more correct — they simply draw the boundaries differently.
