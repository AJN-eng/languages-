Plains Cree · Chapter 1

Sounds & the Writing System

Standard Roman Orthography (SRO)

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and pronounce the three short vowels of Plains Cree
  • Recognize long vowels and understand that vowel length changes meaning
  • Read basic words written in SRO
  • Understand the difference between SRO and Syllabics
The Vowels
Short Vowels

Plains Cree has three short vowels: a (like "but"), i (like "bit"), and o (like "bone" but shorter). They are pronounced lightly and quickly.

Long Vowels

Each short vowel has a long counterpart marked with a macron: ā, ī, ō — plus one extra long vowel ê. Long vowels are held roughly twice as long as short ones. This distinction is meaningful — it changes word meaning.

Example: nipa (he/she sleeps) vs nipā (he/she is dying). The length matters.

Vowel Reference
a
as in "cut" — short
ā
as in "father" — long
i
as in "bit" — short
ī
as in "feet" — long
o
as in "note" — short
ō
as in "road" — long
ê
as in "they" — long only
Key Consonant: the letter C
Pronunciation

In SRO, the letter c is always pronounced like "ch" in "church" — never like the "c" in "cat." This is one of the most important things to remember when reading Cree. Example: mîciwin (food) is pronounced "mee-chi-win."

SRO vs Syllabics
Two writing systems: Plains Cree can be written in Standard Roman Orthography (SRO) — the alphabet used throughout this site — or in Syllabics (ᓀᐦᐃᔭᐍᐏᐣ), a script created in 1840. Both are used by Cree communities. SRO is used here because it is more accessible to beginners learning to read.

Dialogue 2.1 — Meeting Someone
Source: Okimāsis (2004) / Ratt (2016) — adapted for learners
A:
Tânisi.
Hello, how are you?
B:
Namôya nânitaw. Kiya mâka?
Fine. And you?
A:
Pêyakwan. Sarah nitisiyihkâson. Kiya mâka, tânisi kitisiyihkâson?
The same. My name is Sarah. And you, what is your name?
B:
James nitisiyihkâson. Tânitê ohci kiya?
My name is James. Where are you from?
A:
Maskwacîs ohci niya.
I am from Maskwacîs.
B:
Oskana kâ-asastêki ohci niya.
I am from Regina ("the place where bones are piled").
Grammar — Word Order

Plains Cree has flexible word order, but notice how nitisiyihkâson places the name before the verb — the opposite of English. The verb carries so much grammatical information in Cree that a separate pronoun is often unnecessary.

Plains Cree · Chapter 3

Nouns & Animacy

The most fundamental distinction in Cree grammar

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the animate / inanimate distinction in Plains Cree
  • Identify animate nouns using the demonstrative awa
  • Identify inanimate nouns using the demonstrative ôma
  • Form basic plural forms for both categories
The Most Important Rule in Cree

All nouns in Plains Cree are classified as either animate or inanimate. This is not simply about biological life — many things Europeans would consider inanimate are animate in Cree, reflecting a worldview in which spiritual life exists throughout nature. This distinction affects nearly every part of the grammar: verb choice, demonstratives, plurals, and more. It must be memorized for each noun.

Animate nouns use the demonstrative awa. Plural adds -ak (or -wak).

Surprising Animate Nouns

The noun asiniy (rock/stone) is animate in Cree — rocks have spirit in the Cree worldview. Do not try to predict animacy from English logic; it must be learned word by word.

Inclusive vs Exclusive "We"

kîyanân = "we — you and I together." nîyanân = "we — but not you." This distinction is grammatically required in Cree and does not exist in English. It must be learned carefully.

Negation

Place namôya before a verb to negate it: namôya nipâw = "he/she does not sleep." For prohibitions ("don't!"), use êkâ.

Possessive Prefixes

Cree kinship terms almost always take a possessive prefix. ni- = my; ki- = your; o- = his/her. So "your mother" is kikâwiy. At beginner level, focus on the ni- forms first — they are the most commonly taught.

Dialogue 6.1 — Talking About Family
Short dialogue — source: Okimāsis (2004)
A:
Awîna awa?
Who is this?
B:
Nôhkom awa.
This is my grandmother.
A:
Takahki!
That's wonderful!

Yes/No Questions with cî

Add the particle cî to any statement to form a yes/no question: nipâw cî? = "Is he/she sleeping?" It can appear after the verb or at the start of the sentence.

Sample Sentence Pairs
Positive, negative, and question forms

✗
