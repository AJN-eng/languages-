Nakoda · Chapter 1 — Verified

Sounds & the Writing System

SEA standardized orthography — developed with 50+ Îyârhe Nakoda elders

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize the vowels used in the SEA Nakoda writing system
  • Understand the difference between short and long vowels
  • Identify the SEA digraph consonants (ch, th, rh, sh)
  • Understand what a polysynthetic language is
About the writing system: The orthography here is the standardized Stoney Nakoda system developed by the Stoney Education Authority (SEA) and The Language Conservancy (2019–2021), with 50+ Îyârhe Nakoda elders from the Bearspaw, Chiniki, and Wesley First Nations. The SEA uses digraphs — two-letter combinations (ch, th, rh, sh) — rather than special characters. This is the authoritative, community-approved standard as seen at dictionary.stoneynakoda.org.
Vowels
Short and Long Vowels

Nakoda has four vowel qualities, each with a short and long form. Long vowels are marked with a circumflex (^): â, î, û, ô. As in Plains Cree, vowel length is meaningful — it can change word meaning.

SEA Digraph Consonants
Digraphs

The SEA orthography uses digraphs — pairs of letters representing a single sound. The main ones are: ch (like "ch" in "church"), th (like "th" in "the"), sh (like "sh" in "shore"), and rh (a breathy r-sound specific to Nakoda). These are different from the Czech-style characters (č, š) sometimes used in older linguistic documentation — the SEA digraph system is the current community standard.

What is a Polysynthetic Language?
Polysynthetic Structure

Nakoda is a polysynthetic language — a single word can express what takes an entire English sentence. Prefixes, suffixes, and infixes attach to a root to carry subject, object, tense, direction, and more — all within one word. This is a fundamentally different structure from English. Beginners should focus on individual words and short fixed phrases before attempting full sentences.

About Nakoda Greetings

Hâu is the general greeting used across community contexts. Ecén functions both as "good/okay" and as a response to "how are you?" Nakoda does not use the same range of time-of-day greetings (good morning / good evening) that English speakers might expect — Hâu covers most greeting situations.

About the Language

Nakoda (Stoney / Îethka) is a Siouan language — related to Lakota and Dakota, but mutually unintelligible with them. Nakoda means "the friendly ones" or "allies." It is spoken by the Îyârhe Nakoda (Stoney) people in Morley, Bighorn, and Eden Valley, Alberta — within Treaty 7 territory.

Numbers 7 and 8

Notice that 7 (Shakówi) and 8 (Shakónuba) both begin with shako- — 8 appears to be "shako + 2 (núba)," showing internal structure similar to the subtractive logic seen in Plains Cree numbers.

Teen Numbers — agé Pattern
How Teens Work

Teen numbers are formed by placing agé before the unit number: agé wãzhi = 11, agé núba = 12, agé yamní = 13, and so on through 19.
